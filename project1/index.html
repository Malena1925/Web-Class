A Review

About Jon Klassen

Jon Klassen is an award-winning author and illustrator. After starting in the animation industry, he began crafting picture books. His first book that he wrote and illustrated was I Want My Hat Back. Klassen also has also illustrated multiple bestsellers with author Mac Barnett, with whom he runs a picture book blog. Originally from Ontario, Canada, Klassen now lives in Los Angeles. In addition to illustrating books, he is part of Home Home, a collaborative project with his mother, who hand makes felt pieces based on his designs.

About the Book

I Want My Hat Back, published in by Candlewick Press in 2011, is the first book of Jon Klassen's Hat Trilogy. It is also Klassen's first book as both author and illustrator. I Want My Hat Back has been nominated for many awards and was chosen as the New York Times Best Illustrated Children's Book of 2011.

Summary

A bear. A lost hat. A search.

A bear has lost his hat. He really likes his hat. It is red and pointy. He misses his hat, so he sets off to find it. He politely and patiently asks all the animals one by one if they have seen his hat, but everyone says no (some more elaborately than others). A rabbit wearing a pointy red hat defensively says that he has not seen any hats today and to stop questioning him. Without his hat, the bear is despondent until a deer asks him what his hat looks like. This simple question restarts the bear's search with a vengeance.

The Characters

My Review: 5/5 hats

Told completely in dialogue complimented by witty illustrations, I Want My Hat Back is an instant classic. Klassen's use of repetitive language and visual humor tells an explicit narrative with implicit details that engages readers of all ages. While some audiences might not agree with the dark twist ending in a children's book, I appreciated its wicked charm. The simple narrative of a bear losing his hat and wanting it back lends itself to Klassen's signature brilliant dry humor. His illustration style, comprised of simplified shapes and muted colors with minimal backgrounds, compliments the story well and contains so much information and narrative power. The animals' expressions, as Klassen himself described them, make the characters seem as if they don't really know why they're there in the first place, which adds to the comedic effect.
